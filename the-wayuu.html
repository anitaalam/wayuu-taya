Our People

The Wayuu

An indigenous people with a rich heritage spanning centuries, living in the La Guajira peninsula between Venezuela and Colombia.

Scroll
Who Are the Wayuu?

The Wayuu are the largest indigenous

group in both Colombia and Venezuela,

with an estimated population of over

500,000 people.

They have inhabited the arid La Guajira

peninsula for thousands of years,

maintaining their own language, laws,

and traditions despite colonization and

modernity.

Traditions & Heritage

Culture of the Wayuu

Weaving

Wayuu women are master weavers. Their iconic mochilas (handbags) and chinchorro (hammocks) feature geometric patterns called kanasü, each carrying stories and symbolism passed down through generations.

Social Structure

The Wayuu follow a matrilineal system where lineage and inheritance pass through the mother's side. Women play a central role in family leadership, conflict resolution, and cultural preservation.

Music & Dance

The Yonna dance is a sacred tradition performed during celebrations and ceremonies. Accompanied by drums and wind instruments, it represents the connection between the Wayuu and nature.

Spirituality

Dreams hold profound meaning in Wayuu culture. The outsü (spiritual guide) interprets dreams and serves as a healer. The Wayuu maintain deep respect for the land, rain, and natural elements.

Wayuunaiki

A Living Language

Wayuunaiki is an Arawakan language spoken by the Wayuu people. It carries centuries of oral tradition, poetry, and communal knowledge. The Foundation works to preserve and promote Wayuunaiki alongside education programs.

The Reality

Challenges Facing the Wayuu

01

Water Scarcity

La Guajira is one of the driest regions in South America. Many communities walk hours to access water, which is often contaminated.

02

Malnutrition

Child malnutrition rates remain alarmingly high. Limited agricultural land and extreme heat make food production incredibly difficult.

03

Healthcare Access

Remote communities are far from medical facilities. The foundation delivers medicine, health screenings, and essential supplies.

04

Education

Many Wayuu children lack access to schools. Language barriers, distance, and poverty keep thousands from formal education.
